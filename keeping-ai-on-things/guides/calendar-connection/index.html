Connect Your Calendar to Your AI Assistant

An assistant that can see your week can brief it, protect it, and make sure every appointment gets a follow-up.

Level 2 · Connected · 20-30 minutes, one time

What this does

  • A morning brief: today's appointments, what each one needs from you, and the gaps where real work can happen.
  • Showing-day plans: a sensible route and time blocks for a multi-stop day, with buffers you'll actually keep.
  • Follow-up scaffolding: every client appointment automatically gets a "what's the next touch" answer instead of relying on memory.

Pick your path

Path A: Direct connection

  1. In your assistant's settings, open connectors or integrations and look for your calendar (the major assistants support the big calendar platforms; examples, not endorsements: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini with Google and Microsoft calendars).
  2. Connect it and grant access. Start with read access only if the tool offers the choice.
  3. Test: ask "what's on my calendar Thursday?" and check it against the real thing.
  4. Note which assistant tier you're on: calendar connectors are typically paid-tier features, and business tiers keep your data out of model training.

Path B: The paste (no setup, works today)

Open your calendar's week view, copy it (or screenshot it into the chat), and run the same prompts below. Ninety percent of the value, zero connection.

The prompts

The Morning Brief

Save in your workspace, run with coffee.

Look at my calendar for today (or use what I paste below). Give me:

1. THE DAY AT A GLANCE: each appointment with one line on what it is and what I need to walk in with (documents, comps, keys, lockbox codes noted in the event).
2. PREP FLAGS: anything that needs work BEFORE the meeting that isn't done or scheduled.
3. THE GAPS: my open blocks, with a recommendation for the single best use of the biggest one (prospecting, follow-up, or admin) based on what this week looks like.
4. TOMORROW'S ONE THING: what I should set up today so tomorrow starts easy.

Keep it under 200 words. I'm reading this on my phone.

The Showing-Day Planner

I'm building a showing day. Here are the stops and constraints:

SHOWINGS: [address or area + any time restrictions for each]
CLIENT: [name, what they care about, energy level for a long day]
MY CONSTRAINTS: [start/end time, lunch, school pickup, etc.]

Plan the day: a stop order that minimizes backtracking (group by area; I'll gut-check drive times), a time block per stop with a 15-minute buffer every 2-3 stops, and a one-line reminder of what this client should notice at each property. End with the two questions I should ask the client at the end of the day to move toward a decision.

The Follow-Up Scaffold

Look at my client-facing appointments from the last 2 business days (or the list I paste). For each one, tell me: the natural next touch, the channel, and when it should happen. Then draft the one follow-up message that's most overdue, in my voice, ready to send after my review.

Watch-outs

  • Reading is not writing. Letting the assistant see your calendar is low-risk. Letting it create or move events deserves a two-week trial period first, and you review every invite before it sends.
  • Mind what lives in event titles. If your calendar events say "Smith divorce sale, 4012 Maple," a connected assistant reads that too. Keep sensitive detail in your CRM, not in event names.
  • Time math deserves a glance. AI is good at planning and occasionally bad at arithmetic. Sanity-check drive-time assumptions on tight days.
