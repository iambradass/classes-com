Most major real estate CRMs shipped AI features in the last two years: smart follow-up suggestions, message drafting, lead summaries. Fastest path, zero setup risk, usually the least flexible.

  1. 1

    Search your CRM's settings for "AI"

    Check the settings and help center. You may already be paying for it.

  2. 2

    Turn it on and test

    Run it against one real lead and judge the output honestly.

  3. 3

    Split the jobs

    Keep your general assistant for everything the built-in AI can't do (voice, strategy, content). Fine to run this alongside Path B or C.

The big assistants (examples, not endorsements: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) all support "connectors" or "integrations" that link outside apps into your chats. Middleware automation tools can bridge assistants to hundreds of CRMs the assistants don't support natively.

What the connectors screen looks like in most assistants: search for your CRM by name, sign in with your CRM login, done.

  1. 1

    Look for your CRM in the connectors list

    In your assistant's settings, open the connectors or integrations screen and search for your CRM by name. If it's listed, connect it with your CRM login and you're done.

  2. 2

    No listing? Bridge it

    Check whether your CRM connects to a middleware automation platform (the tools that link app A to app B). The common recipe: assistant drafts, middleware pushes contacts, notes, and tasks into the CRM.

  3. 3

    Start read-only

    Let the assistant read your CRM for two weeks before you allow it to create or edit anything.

  4. 4

    Test with a fake contact

    Create "Test Buyer" in the CRM, then ask the assistant to find them, summarize them, and add a note. Verify in the CRM that the note landed where you expected.

No connector? No problem. CSV out, work in the assistant, CSV back in. Works with every CRM ever made and gets you 80% of the value of a live connection with zero setup.

The prompt

The Database AnalystSave it in your workspace; run it every Monday
database-analyst.prompt
You are my database analyst with access to my CRM. Every time I run this, do the following:

1. Who needs me today: the 5 contacts with the highest urgency to hear from me, and why. Urgency = active deals first, then time-sensitive notes, then longest silence among past clients.
2. What changed: any new leads, new notes, or status changes since last week worth knowing about.
3. One risk: a contact or deal that looks like it's slipping through the cracks.

Format it as a brief I can read in 2 minutes. Do not draft messages unless I ask. If you can't read a field or the connection fails, say so plainly instead of working from memory.

Watch-outs

Brokerage policy first. Connecting client data to an outside tool is exactly what data policies are written about. Ask before you connect.

Training off, business tier on. Never connect a CRM through a free consumer account. Use a paid tier with model training disabled.

Read before write. An assistant that mis-reads is annoying. An assistant that mis-writes into 3,000 contact records is a weekend. Grant write access only after read access has proven reliable.

The assistant summarizes; the CRM is truth. When numbers matter (deal terms, dates), open the record.
