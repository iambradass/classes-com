Keeping AI on Things · Setup Guide

Connect Your CRM to Your AI Assistant

Get your assistant and your CRM (client database) talking, so follow-up plans, dossiers, and campaigns land where you actually work.

Level 2 · Connected · 30-60 minutes, one time

What this does

  • Lets you ask questions of your own database: "who haven't I talked to in 90 days," "which leads mentioned relocating," "build today's call list."
  • Turns the dossiers and task lists your assistant writes into records and tasks inside the CRM, not text you re-type.
  • Unlocks the connected versions of every other playbook on the hub.

Pick your path

There are three ways in. Check them in this order and stop at the first one that works for you.

Path A: Your CRM's built-in AI

Most major real estate CRMs shipped AI features in the last two years: smart follow-up suggestions, message drafting, lead summaries.

  1. Search your CRM's settings and help center for "AI." You may already be paying for it.
  2. Turn it on and test it against one real lead.
  3. Keep your general assistant for everything the built-in AI can't do (voice, strategy, content), and let the CRM's AI handle in-app suggestions.

Fastest path, zero setup risk, but usually the least flexible. Fine to run this alongside Path B or C.

Path B: A direct connection

The big assistants (examples, not endorsements: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) all support "connectors" or "integrations" that link outside apps into your chats. Middleware automation tools can bridge assistants to hundreds of CRMs the assistants don't support natively.

  1. In your assistant's settings, open the connectors or integrations screen and search for your CRM by name. If it's listed, connect it with your CRM login and you're done.
  2. If it's not listed, check whether your CRM connects to a middleware automation platform (the tools that link app A to app B). If so, the common recipe is: assistant drafts, middleware pushes contacts, notes, and tasks into the CRM.
  3. Start read-only if the option exists. Let the assistant read your CRM for two weeks before you allow it to create or edit anything.
  4. Test with a fake contact first: create "Test Buyer" in the CRM, then ask the assistant to find them, summarize them, and add a note. Verify in the CRM that the note landed where you expected.

Path C: The export loop (works with every CRM ever made)

No connector? No problem. CSV out, work in the assistant, CSV back in.

This is its own playbook with prompts ready to go: Turn a CSV Export Into a Campaign. Run it monthly and you get 80% of the value of a live connection with zero setup.

The prompt

The Database Analyst

Once connected (Path B), save this in your workspace and run it every Monday.

You are my database analyst with access to my CRM. Every time I run this, do the following:

1. Who needs me today: the 5 contacts with the highest urgency to hear from me, and why. Urgency = active deals first, then time-sensitive notes, then longest silence among past clients.
2. What changed: any new leads, new notes, or status changes since last week worth knowing about.
3. One risk: a contact or deal that looks like it's slipping through the cracks.

Format it as a brief I can read in 2 minutes. Do not draft messages unless I ask. If you can't read a field or the connection fails, say so plainly instead of working from memory.

Watch-outs

  • Brokerage policy first. Connecting client data to an outside tool is exactly what data policies are written about. Ask before you connect.
  • Training off, business tier on. Never connect a CRM through a free consumer account. Use a paid tier with model training disabled.
  • Read before write. An assistant that mis-reads is annoying. An assistant that mis-writes into 3,000 contact records is a weekend. Grant write access only after read access has proven reliable.
  • The assistant summarizes, the CRM is truth. When numbers matter (deal terms, dates), open the record.
