Neighborhood Landing Pages

A dedicated page for your farm neighborhood, built by your assistant in one afternoon, that gives every postcard and sign QR code somewhere worth landing.

45-60 minutes QR-ready

What this does

One polished page about one neighborhood: what makes it special, a current market snapshot, and a "what's my home worth" call to action that feeds your CRM (client database).

Gives your farm a destination. A QR code on a postcard that opens a page about their street outperforms one that opens your generic homepage.

Costs nothing but time. Modern AI assistants write complete, working web pages; you supply the local knowledge.

What you'll need

Real facts about the neighborhood: your recent market numbers (from the MLS), amenities, and what residents actually love about it. Ten minutes of notes.

Somewhere to host the page. In order of ease: a landing-page or funnel builder inside your CRM, your website provider's "add a page" feature, or a simple static-hosting service. Your assistant can walk you through whichever one you name.

Your AI Second Brain workspace.

The setup

  1. 1

    Gather your inputs

    Neighborhood name, 5 amenity/lifestyle facts, 3 recent market numbers with dates, your contact info and photo, and where leads should go.

  2. 2

    Run the Page Builder prompt

    Ask for the result as a single, complete HTML file.

  3. 3

    Preview it

    Save the file and open it in your browser. It works right off your desktop. Check it on your phone: most visitors arrive from a QR scan.

  4. 4

    Iterate in plain English

    "Make the header taller," "warmer colors," "shorter intro." The assistant edits, you re-preview. Two or three rounds gets it right.

  5. 5

    Wire the form

    Easiest reliable route: build a form in your CRM, copy its embed code or link, and have the assistant place it in the page. Now every lead lands in your database with tags and automation.

  6. 6

    Publish and print

    Publish on your chosen host, generate a QR code for the page URL, and put it on your farm postcards, door hangers, and open-house signs.

  7. 7

    Update quarterly

    Stale market numbers on a farming page quietly tell the neighborhood you stopped paying attention. Calendar reminder, 15 minutes.

The prompt

page-builder.prompt
You are my web developer and copywriter. Build me a complete, single-file HTML landing page for a neighborhood I farm. Everything (styles, scripts) inline in one file so I can preview it by opening the file in a browser.

NEIGHBORHOOD: [name, city]
WHAT RESIDENTS LOVE (my notes): [5 bullets: parks, schools zone info stated neutrally, walkability, architecture, community events]
MARKET SNAPSHOT (my MLS numbers, with dates): [median sale price, days on market, homes sold last quarter]
ABOUT ME: [name, brokerage, phone, email, one-line positioning]
LEAD CAPTURE: [paste your CRM form embed code, or say "placeholder form" and I'll wire it later]

Requirements:
- Mobile-first. Most visitors scan a QR code from print. Large text, big tap targets, fast load, no images I have to source (use color and typography instead; leave clearly-marked placeholder spots where I can add my own photos).
- Sections: hero with the neighborhood name and a one-line hook, "living here" highlights, market snapshot with a "numbers as of [date]" line, a home-value call to action with the lead form, and a short about-me footer.
- Tone: warm local expert, zero hype. This page is about the neighborhood, not about me.
- Fair-housing safe: describe the place, homes, and lifestyle. Never describe the people who live there or who "should" live there, and no demographic language of any kind.
- Include a title tag and meta description for search.

Give me the complete file, then a bulleted list of exactly what I should customize before publishing.

Watch-outs

Fair housing applies to farming pages twice: the page copy AND any advertising you run to promote it. Describe property and place, keep ad targeting broad, document what you ran.

Source your numbers. Every market stat on the page needs a date and a source you could show if asked. "Numbers as of June 2026, [your MLS]" is the habit.

Test the form before printing anything. Submit it yourself, confirm the lead shows up in your CRM, then order postcards. Not the other way around.

Brokerage advertising rules still apply: required broker identification, license disclosures, and ad review policies cover this page like any other ad.
